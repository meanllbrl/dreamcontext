## 1. 🆕 Auction overlap as a distinct mechanism (NEW concept in corpus)

The first time anyone in the corpus distinguishes auction overlap from audience overlap, with a mechanistic explanation for why consolidation matters.

**Verbatim — the disambiguation:**
> "There's some misconceptions around overlap. A lot of people think I'm talking about audience overlap. When I say auction overlap, I'm not… audience overlap would be you've got two different campaigns, two different adsets, and they're targeting a lot of the same audience. Now, that's not ideal, but the whole idea of that's going to increase the cost because you're bidding against yourself is a bit silly. There are thousands of advertisers trying to reach the same people. One extra campaign ad set… not going to make much difference." `[06:23]`

**Verbatim — what auction overlap actually is:**
> "Meta systems are now very sophisticated at working out what ad impression frequency is required to get the outcome that you want. So Meta might work out, you know what, we're better off at generating four or five ad impressions for a single individual over a 24, 48 hour time period as opposed to spreading out those ad impressions over a week. Well, Meta could plan that really well if it's just got the one campaign, one adset targeting a person… If you've got some ad impressions coming from one adset, some ad impressions coming from another adset or different campaigns, much more difficult for Meta to work out that delivery system." `[06:56]`

**Pattern shape extracted:**
- Audience overlap = same people get bid on by two of your campaigns. Ben says: largely overstated as a problem.
- Auction overlap = same person gets *delivery decisions* from multiple campaigns/adsets, breaking Meta's per-user frequency-planning over a 24–48h window.
- The **real** reason consolidation works: Meta can plan delivery frequency to one individual ONLY if delivery to that individual is owned by ONE adset.

**Why this matters for the corpus:**
This is the first mechanistic answer to the "why consolidate?" question. Charlie's `_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` answer was about *learning-phase math* + *similarity beats scale*. Ben's auction-overlap framing is *delivery-frequency planning*. Both can be true; both are different mechanisms; both reinforce the same default behaviour (one campaign / one adset until volume earns more).

**Status:** single-speaker (Ben). Distinct mechanism, no second source yet. Treat as a *complementary* explanation to Charlie's reasoning, not as independent corroboration of the same claim.

## 2. 🚨 NEW critical anti-pattern — don't kill the top-funnel ad

The first guard in the corpus against naive "kill the loser" optimisation.

**Setup (verbatim):**
> "We will create some ads thinking these are more likely to be top of funnel ads. We will create some that more like to be middle of funnel and then some that are going to be bottom of funnel… Meta is now sophisticated enough to work that out. So they will add sequence for you automatically. They will use some ads initially put in front of people first off, then other ads in more of a retargeting capacity." `[16:30]`

**The detection signal (verbatim):**
> "What you'll often see in those businesses that see this automatic ad sequencing taking place within the one adset is that some ads will consistently have money spent on them, but they don't have many conversions. Looks like their cost per lead or cost per sale isn't very good. And you think, why is Meta doing this? It's because they're using that in a top of funnel capacity and then using other ads to retarget and get the conversion." `[17:51]`

**The hard guard (verbatim):**
> "You don't want to make the mistake of turning off the ad that is being used in a top funnel capacity because then your other ad performance will massively worsen because it isn't benefiting from that other ad being used in a top funnel capacity." `[18:22]`

**When this happens (verbatim):**
> "The ad accounts where this happens more are ones where the ask is larger. So if you're selling something that has a higher price point or you're selling a service that's more involved, people need more steps to get to the point where they're willing to inquire, buy, etc. If you're selling an inexpensive e-commerce product, it's going to be much more just directly buy it." `[17:30]`

**Pattern shape extracted:**
- Inside a single high-volume adset with mixed creative types (TOFU / MOFU / BOFU all together), Meta will internally route ads to different journey stages.
- An ad with high spend + low direct conversions is *not* automatically a loser — it may be carrying the awareness/familiarity load for the converters.
- Higher-ticket / higher-consideration offers exhibit this more.
- Inexpensive impulse-buy ecom: less likely; "kill the loser" heuristic still ~works.

**Implication for the eventual Strategy Optimizer:**
Before recommending "pause this ad — bad CPL/CPS", the agent must:
1. Check if the ad sits inside a single-adset structure with mixed creative types.
2. Check if the offer is high-consideration (ask the operator).
3. If both yes: refuse the auto-pause recommendation. Surface the auction-overlap + sequencing argument instead, and ask the operator to confirm a *manual* pause if they really want it.

**Status:** single-source (Ben). Important enough to flag now; need 1 non-Ben source to promote to a hard rule.

**Potential conflict with Charlie's "test purpose = improve the weakest ad in control"** (`_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` §2): Charlie's framing says *improve* the worst ad, not *kill* it — so they're aligned in spirit. But Charlie's "did spend redistribute and did campaign perf improve" rubric (`_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` §4) could mistakenly read a load-bearing-but-low-converting ad as a "test failure" if the spend redistribution kills it. Worth flagging in the eventual playbook.

## 3. New corroboration of Charlie via different framing

**Claim:** stop testing audience permutations (interest vs lookalike vs open, different ICPs in different adsets).

**Charlie's framing** (`_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` §7): "similarity beats scale" — focus on a small number of ICPs that behave similarly, get thousands of them.

**Ben's framing here (verbatim):**
> "We very rarely now test different audiences at the adset or even the campaign level… for the same reason that we don't separate [cold and warm] out, Meta is kind of going to ignore a lot of your suggestions… is why we won't test different interest targeting options in different adsets or interest targeting versus lookalike versus open targeting. We used to do a lot of that sort of stuff. We don't do that anymore because all of that falls within the suggested audience section unless it's going to do its own thing." `[07:51]`

**Cross-source state:** 2 distinct speakers (Ben + Charlie), 2 different mechanistic explanations, 1 shared anti-pattern (don't fragment by audience-permutation testing). **Promotion-eligible.**

**Exception both speakers respect:** **location-based testing**, because location is in the *control* section (hard boundary). Ben verbatim: *"It's not a coincidence that we will test different targeting options when it comes to a location basis across different adsets, maybe different campaigns and the location targeting falls within the control section. Like that is not a coincidence."* `[09:11]`

This exception is itself a corroboration of the controls-vs-suggestions framework already at 2-speaker status from prior videos.

## 5. Net-new sub-claims worth recording (single-speaker, corpus-novel)

These are smaller-scope claims net-new to the corpus from this video. None reach the 2-speaker bar; all stay as single-speaker observations.

### 5.1 1 campaign per product/service *range*, not per *variant*

> "By default, I like to use one campaign per product or service range. So, we're not going to have a different campaign for red hats versus blue hats. We are going to have a different campaign for hats versus shoes because those campaigns we want to optimize differently. We want meta to treat them as as we might need to target slightly different people. We might need to advertise them in a different way. People might need more convincing to buy shoes than a hat." `[18:50]`

**Pattern shape:** the granularity of campaign separation is *category*, not *SKU* or *colourway*. Operational benefit (verbatim): *"All right, we're out of stock on this. Turn this off… We're at capacity for this service. Turn this off."* `[20:14]`

**Exception:** dynamic-catalog campaigns — small budget, mostly retargeting → can span product ranges across the whole catalogue. `[19:25]`

### 5.2 Funnel-stage logic moves from campaigns to creative types within ONE adset

The old TOFU/MOFU/BOFU campaign separation is replaced by mixing creative *types* in a single adset and letting Meta sequence them.

**Verbatim creative-type taxonomy from this video:**
- **Top-of-funnel:** founder-led, story-based ("why we created the company")
- **Middle-of-funnel:** product demonstration
- **Bottom-of-funnel:** customer testimonial, strong CTA

**Pattern shape:** when scoping creative briefs, ensure a mix of all three types ships into the same adset, not into separate campaigns.

### 5.3 Ad-copy testing inside the ad (sharper articulation than `_youtube__dAJyqo6wnq4` §17)

The variation system isn't just *creative diversity for personalisation* (the framing in the foundational video). It's *the correct testing surface for headline / primary text / description variants*. Anti-pattern (verbatim):
> "If you've got all this different ad creative and then let's say for each ad creative you needed three or four different ads to test different headline variations, you very quickly get into just silly numbers of ads and too difficult for you as the advertiser to be able to monitor that and keep up with it." `[13:25]`

**Implication:** the eventual brief format treats `headline / primary_text / description` as 1-of-N arrays per ad, never as separate ads.

### 5.4 Single-adset structure breaks creative testing (where "deviate" is permitted)

> "A lot of ad accounts run into the issue when they just have the one campaign, one ad set where they really struggle to test new ad creative… I try and introduce new ads… meta doesn't spend any money on them. They just spend money on my previous best performers, but those are slowly fatiguing and performance is dropping." `[22:31]`

**This is the same gap Charlie identified** (`_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` §1–§5). Ben's resolution: switch to the creative testing tool. Charlie's resolution: control + test adsets in the same campaign.

→ **The Ben-vs-Charlie testing-methodology conflict from `_youtube__E_wZJhuSK5U` §12.2 remains unresolved.** This video reaffirms Ben's side without addressing Charlie's. Tie-breaker still pending a 3rd distinct speaker.
